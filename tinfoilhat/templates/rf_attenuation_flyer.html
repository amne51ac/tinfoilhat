ABOUT RF ATTENUATION

RF ATTENUATION BASICS

RF attenuation measures how effectively a material reduces radio frequency signals, expressed in decibels (dB). Positive values indicate signal blocking, negative values indicate amplification. We measure this by comparing signal strength before and after passing through shielding material.

FARADAY SHIELDING

A Faraday shield is a conductive enclosure that blocks external electric fields by redistributing electric charges. For optimal shielding, the material must be conductive, continuous (no gaps), and ideally grounded. Any opening larger than 1/10th of the wavelength allows significant signal leakage. Multiple small holes are better than one large hole of the same total area.

THE SCIENCE BEHIND IT

When an electromagnetic wave hits a conductive material like aluminum foil, it induces an electric current that creates its own opposing electromagnetic field. Shielding effectiveness depends on material conductivity, thickness, frequency, and the presence of gaps. Higher frequencies are more difficult to shield effectively.

DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS

Building an effective RF shield requires balancing ventilation, visibility, comfort, and style. Use multiple layers of foil with good contact between layers, minimize gaps and seams, and use conductive tape to seal any openings. Consider that gaps affecting WiFi (2.4 GHz, λ ≈ 12.5 cm) might not impact FM radio (88.5 MHz, λ ≈ 3.4 meters).

PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS

RF shielding has many practical uses: RF-blocking wallets protect credit cards from wireless skimming, Faraday cages prevent interference in medical imaging rooms, and RF shielding bags protect electronic devices during forensic analysis.
